Interests

A few things I care about. Click any item to read more.

  • Film Photography Canon AE-1 +
    I shoot on a Canon AE-1 that I found in my dad's basement. There's something irreplaceable about the ritual — loading a roll, committing to each frame, then waiting weeks to see what you captured. My film photography page goes deeper on this.
  • Running Pittsburgh trails +
    Running clears my head in a way nothing else does. I mostly run trails and roads around Pittsburgh. Frick Park is a favorite. I track everything on Strava and love having a log of where I've been.
  • Tinkering & Making mechanical +
    Being a mechanical engineer by day means I can't always turn off the urge to fix, build, or improve things. I like taking apart old gear, understanding how things are put together, and occasionally making something new from scratch.
  • Travel with a camera +
    I try to bring my camera everywhere. Recent highlights: summiting the Zugspitze in Germany, hiking the Cathedral Cove in New Zealand, a lake in Austria. The goal is to collect places, not just visit them.
  • Cooking weeknight experiments +
    Cooking is one of those hobbies where the feedback loop is immediate and delicious. I like trying to recreate things I've eaten while traveling, and I'm slowly getting better at not burning things.
  • Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance Robert Pirsig +
    The book that made me think seriously about the relationship between technology and the people who use it. Pirsig's idea of Quality — something you can't fully define but immediately recognize — stuck with me. Required reading if you make things for a living.
  • The Road Cormac McCarthy +
    Bleak, beautiful, and impossible to put down. McCarthy strips language down to almost nothing and it somehow becomes more powerful for it. I read it in one sitting and felt wrecked afterward in the best way.
  • A Gentleman in Moscow Amor Towles +
    One of the most purely enjoyable novels I've read. Count Rostov is under house arrest in a Moscow hotel for decades, and somehow Towles makes it feel like an adventure. A reminder that a rich inner life can survive almost any constraint.
  • The Pragmatic Programmer Thomas & Hunt +
    The engineering mindset translated into software, and a lot of it applies back to physical engineering too. The idea of being a craftsperson — caring about your tools and your work — resonated with me immediately.
  • Endurance Alfred Lansing +
    Shackleton's failed Antarctic expedition, told with documentary precision. Twenty-seven men survive two years trapped in the ice through sheer collective willpower. I've never felt cold reading a book before.
  • Illinois Sufjan Stevens · 2005 +
    One of those albums I return to every year and find something new in it. Stevens stuffed this record with orchestral maximalism and raw grief simultaneously, and it somehow works. "Casimir Pulaski Day" is devastating.
  • In Rainbows Radiohead · 2007 +
    The warmest Radiohead record — which isn't saying much, but it's genuinely beautiful. "Reckoner" and "Videotape" are perfect. I go back to this constantly while running or on long flights.
  • Either/Or Elliott Smith · 1997 +
    Recorded almost entirely alone on a four-track in his apartment. Every song feels like something overheard rather than performed. The lo-fi production is part of it — it couldn't exist any other way.
  • Funeral Arcade Fire · 2004 +
    A debut record that felt like it arrived fully-formed. Big, anthemic, earnest in a way that could have been embarrassing but never is. "Neighborhood #1 (Tunnels)" is one of the greatest album openers ever made.
  • Blue Joni Mitchell · 1971 +
    As exposed and vulnerable as a record gets. Mitchell said she was "wide open" when she made it, and you can hear it. Something about the open guitar tunings and her voice makes it feel like a private journal you're not supposed to be reading.
  • Carrie & Lowell Sufjan Stevens · 2015 +
    The opposite of Illinois in scale — just fingerpicked guitar, hushed vocals, grief. Made after his mother died. It's one of those records that's difficult to listen to but impossible not to return to.
